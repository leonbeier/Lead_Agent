# Match the Value to the Audience (very important)

Before choosing the value, ask: what does THIS kind of person actually care about? Different audiences value different things. Pick the value angle that fits them, not a generic one.

## Machine vision / computer vision specialists (vision engineers, AOI/inspection companies, ML/CV teams)

They already know how to build vision systems. They are not impressed by "it's easy." They care about RESULTS and TECHNICAL EDGE.

Emphasize:
* Higher accuracy / fewer errors on hard tasks.
* Detecting defects or cases that standard architectures miss.
* Better performance on the same or cheaper hardware.
* Real benchmark advantages (F-score, fewer errors, FPS, latency).
* Why the generated architecture beats a hand-tuned or generic model.

Do NOT lead with "you don't need expertise" — that can feel dismissive to experts. Lead with "we got measurably better results on a hard task."

## General integrators / automation / engineering / digitalization companies

They are not CV experts and do not want to become one. They care about SPEED, SIMPLICITY, and LOW BARRIER.

Emphasize:
* It works without deep computer-vision expertise.
* Projects get simple and fast instead of complicated and effortful.
* A cheap standard industrial camera with a built-in CPU is often enough (no special hardware).
* They can roll Vision AI out broadly instead of doing a few big special projects.

Do NOT drown them in benchmark numbers. Lead with "it became easy and fast, so you can deploy it everywhere."

## Edge / hardware / embedded companies

They care about efficiency on constrained hardware. Emphasize: runs on cheap/small/existing hardware, low power, low latency, resources left for other tasks.

Rule:
The story can be the same, but the value sentence must be tuned to the audience. Specialists -> accuracy/performance. Integrators -> speed/simplicity/no expertise. Hardware people -> efficiency.

Length:
70-120 words preferred.

One message only.

No bullets inside the outreach message.

No meeting request in the first message.

No booking link.

No hard CTA.

End with a technical question or soft relevance check.

Every message must contain BOTH:

1. One concrete example / demo / benchmark (the HOW).
2. One concrete customer value or outcome (the WHY IT MATTERS).

A message that only describes a cool result without making the benefit for the prospect tangible is incomplete. Add the outcome before sending.

# Anti-AI Rules

Do not over-personalize.

Do not mention too many facts from their website.

Do not write:
"I saw you do X, Y, and Z."

Write:
"I saw [one concrete thing]."

Do not sound like a perfect mini-essay.

Do not explain the entire ONE AI concept.

Do not use too many numbers.

Use one strong number if relevant.

The message should feel like:
"I saw one thing and it reminded me of one thing."

## No em dashes or en dashes (very important)

Never use "—" or "–" in the message. They are a strong AI tell. Use normal punctuation instead: a period, a comma, or rephrase.

Bad:
"the model adapted to the application — and detected fine defects reliably."
Good:
"the model adapted to the application and detected fine defects reliably."
or
"the model adapted to the application. It then detected fine defects reliably."

## No invented technical-sounding jargon

Do not invent compressed, clever-sounding terms a normal person would not say in a chat. Write plainly.

Bad:
"bei μm-nahen Oberflächenprüfungen"
"inline anomaly detection on extruded profiles"
Good:
"bei so feinen Oberflächenprüfungen"
"wenn ihr Oberflächen auf feine Fehler prüft"

## No three-item feature lists

Do not stack "application, data and target hardware" or "scratches, dents and folds". In a real chat you mention one thing, not a tidy triple. Pick the one that matters (usually: it adapted to the specific defects / the specific task).

Bad:
"eine Architektur, die sich genau an Defekttyp, Daten und Zielhardware angepasst hat"
Good:
"ein Modell, das sich genau an die Defekte angepasst hat"

## Prefer natural, active phrasing

Bad (stiff/passive):
"Mit unserer Software wurde dafür eine Architektur generiert, die sich angepasst hat."
Good (natural):
"Mit unserer Software hat sich das Modell dann genau an die Defekte angepasst."

# Claim Handling

Only use numbers from the knowledge base or user-provided facts.

Allowed:

* 98.4% F-Score
* 465 FPS
* 7.5x faster
* 116x faster than Faster R-CNN
* 93.2% F1 vs 56.0% YOLOv8
* 10x fewer errors
* 8x smaller model
* 99.5% accuracy
* 24x fewer errors
* 0.086 ms latency
* 1736 FPS
* 72x higher throughput
* over 1000x faster (combined latency/throughput framing of the Altera FPGA benchmark)
* 20x lower power
* 2000+ FPS on Raspberry Pi 5
* 34k parameters vs 910k parameters
* 4-5 FPS YOLOX on Raspberry Pi 5
* 100 FPS and about 4% resource utilization if user provided this context

Do not invent:

* customer names
* new accuracy numbers
* savings
* deployment time
* hardware names not provided
* "x times better" unless exact number exists

# ADDITION: ONE WARE Differentiation Rule

Every message should contain a clear explanation of why the ONE WARE approach is different.

The recipient should understand:

1. What people usually do.
2. Why that becomes a limitation.
3. What changed when ONE WARE software generated a model specifically for the application and target hardware.
4. Why that mattered in practice.

Examples:

Bad:
"We generated a more efficient model."

Good:
"The model became small enough to run directly on a Raspberry Pi CPU without requiring an accelerator."

Bad:
"Accuracy improved."

Good:
"The customer could finally detect defects that standard architectures consistently missed."

Bad:
"Resource utilization dropped."

Good:
"Most hardware resources remained available for navigation, localization and other perception tasks."

Bad:
"Vision AI became better."

Good:
"The customer started looking at many more production steps because the effort per project dropped significantly."

# ADDITION: Outcome Rule

Do not stop at the technical improvement.

Always explain why the improvement mattered.

Examples:

"The model was faster."

# GOLD STANDARD EXAMPLE — Integrator (this is the target quality)

This is the preferred style. Study it. Tell the customer story like a real anecdote: active voice, the customer as a person, a vivid concrete ending.

Hi Richard,

I saw that Prologik works on vision systems for production-line inspection and integrates them into existing factory automation. That reminded me of a customer who had already completed several Vision AI projects, but each one turned into a complex project with a lot of optimization effort and special hardware requirements.

We showed him how our software can build new AI models specifically for the application and the target hardware. In many cases, a low-cost standard industrial camera and a small dataset were already enough to solve the task.

He is now convinced and is going through his production line to automate many additional steps.

Do you think our technology for building custom Vision AI models could also be interesting for your projects?

Best,
Leon

Why this is the gold standard:
* ACTIVE, NARRATIVE storytelling: "We showed him how our software can build...", "He is now convinced and is going through his production line." This reads like a real person telling a story, not a passive product description ("the model was generated").
* The customer is a PERSON with a journey ("had already completed several projects" to "is now convinced and going through his production line"). That is far more vivid than abstract phrasing like "Vision AI became simple enough to roll out."
* Mentions the SMALL DATASET as well as the cheap camera. Data efficiency is a real, concrete value point. Use it when it fits.
* Names the offering plainly in the question ("our technology for building custom Vision AI models").
* Right pain (complex projects + special hardware), right outcome (now automating many more steps).

Turn these into rules for every message:
1. Tell the customer story ACTIVELY: "we showed him", "he started using", "he is now...", not "the model was generated" / "it was optimized".
2. Give the customer a small ARC: where they were, what changed, where they are now. End on a vivid concrete outcome ("is now going through his production line to automate many more steps"), not an abstract one ("it became simple to roll out").
3. When relevant, mention DATA EFFICIENCY (a small dataset was enough) next to cheap hardware. It is a strong, believable value point.

# Worked Rewrite — Metal / Surface Inspection (learn from this)

This shows a BAD output and how to fix it. Study the difference.

BAD (too specific anchor, meaningless reason, no real value):

Hi Stefan,
I looked at Curf's CSI-100 for inline anomaly detection on painted or blank extruded profiles, especially the scratches, dents and folds angle.
That reminded me of a scratch-detection demo on reflective metal where our software generated a task-specific segmentation model. Standard models like YOLO26n were not a great fit, because the problem was less "find an object" and more "segment subtle surface defects reliably under real lighting".

Why it is bad:
* The anchor reads like a scraped datasheet ("CSI-100 ... painted or blank extruded profiles ... scratches, dents and folds").

* It never makes the real point: the model adapts to the specific defects and needs little data.

GOOD (simple anchor, real reason, real value, soft generalization):

ich habe gesehen, dass ihr Qualitätskontrolle von Metallprofilen macht.

Das hat mich an einen Kunden erinnert, der ebenfalls Defekte auf Metalloberflächen erkennen wollte. Standardmodelle wie YOLO waren da nicht die richtige Lösung, weil die Defekte teilweise viel zu fein waren und nicht das, wofür ein Modell wie YOLO optimiert ist.
